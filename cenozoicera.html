Cenozoic Era

66 Million Years Ago To Present

The Cenozoic Era, often referred to as the Age of Mammals, marks a significant period in Earth's history characterized by the diversification of mammals and birds after the mass extinction that ended the dinosaurs. It is divided into three periods: Paleogene, Neogene, and Quaternary.

Major Events

Paleogene Period: Following the extinction of the dinosaurs, the Paleogene was a time of recovery and rapid evolution. Mammals grew larger and more diverse, filling ecological niches left vacant. This period saw the rise of early primates, whales, and the ancestors of modern birds.

Neogene Period: The Neogene continued the evolutionary trends of the Paleogene, with mammals like elephants, horses, and large carnivores becoming prominent. Grasslands expanded, leading to the evolution of grazers, while primates continued to diversify, eventually leading to the emergence of the hominids.

Quaternary Period: The most recent period, the Quaternary, is marked by significant climatic changes, including ice ages. This period saw the rise of Homo sapiens and the extinction of many large mammals, such as mammoths and saber-toothed cats, as humans spread across the globe.

Climate

The Cenozoic Era experienced dramatic climatic shifts, including periods of warming and cooling. The Paleogene was generally warm, but the Neogene saw a cooling trend leading to the establishment of the ice ages in the Quaternary. These climatic changes significantly influenced the evolution and distribution of flora and fauna.

Significance

The Cenozoic Era is crucial for understanding modern ecosystems and the evolutionary history of mammals, including humans. It represents a time of significant diversification and adaptation, laying the foundation for contemporary biodiversity. The era's events continue to influence current climate patterns and biological interactions.
